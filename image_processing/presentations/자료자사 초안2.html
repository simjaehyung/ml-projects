Method A (Standard)

Y = 0.299R + 0.587G + 0.114B
Standard Grayscale
Why this looks more "natural":
The human eye is not equally sensitive to all colors (mostly to Green, least to Blue). Method A accounts for human biology by giving Green the highest weight (58.7%) and Blue the lowest (11.4%), creating an image that matches our perceived brightness perfectly.

Method B (Averaging)

I = (R + G + B) / 3
Average Grayscale
Why this falls short:
This method treats all color channels equally. A pure bright yellow will convert to the exact same average gray as a pure deep blue. Because this ignores human perception, the resulting image often looks strangely flattened with incorrect contrast.

Lighting Robustness: Diagonal vs. Axial Scaling

How does changing brightness ($k$) affect the mathematical position of colors in their 3D spaces?

The Math of Brightness

RGB Space (Diagonal vector scaling)
[R', G', B'] = k * [R, G, B] Requires 3 multiplications per pixel. Colors shift diagonally towards the $[255,255,255]$ white corner or the origin $[0,0,0]$.
HSV Space (Axial scalar scaling)
V' = k * V Requires 1 multiplication. Hue (X/Y position) is perfectly preserved. The color simply moves straight up or down the Z-axis.
